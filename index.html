What We Do

Research

Background

Every year 5 million people remain permanently disabled after a stroke due to the brain's limited ability to regenerate damaged tissue. The lack of an effective therapy that promotes long-term recovery after stroke represents a substantial clinical burden and unmet need for medical treatment outside the confines of conventional therapies. Recent advances in cell therapies have shown promising preclinical results in animal stroke models but have not been confirmed beyond doubt in a clinical setting. Major uncertainties remain — e.g., regarding the source of the transplanted cells, immune compatibility to the recipient, and cell survival and functional integration into the damaged neural circuitry.

Research Goals

Our primary goal is to overcome major limitations recognized by the scientists in the field of cell therapies following stroke. We work with our excellent collaborators to optimize the cell source for transplantation with the following focus:

  • Functionalize and target the cells towards specific ischemic stroke environments
  • Develop a cell shuttle system to facilitate the migration of cells across the blood–brain barrier
  • Reduce immune incompatibility to the recipient by interference with the HLA system
  • Preconditioning and hydrogel-embedding of cells to improve graft survival

Main Techniques

To understand and develop novel cell-based therapies, we exploit our expertise in experimental stroke research combined with the use of molecular and genetic tools to optimize the cell source. For instance, we validate successful stroke procedures using laser Doppler flowmetry and utilize bioluminescence imaging to longitudinally track the transplanted cells in vivo. To uncover functional deficits in the stroked mice, we perform state-of-the-art deep learning-based gait analysis at regular intervals. We further plan to in-depth characterize the cellular fate of the transplants using single-cell and spatially resolved transcriptomics.

The People

The Research Team

Principal Investigator

Ruslan Rust

Ruslan is an Assistant Professor of Neuroscience at the University of Southern California (USC). He previously served as a principal investigator at the Institute for Regenerative Medicine at the University of Zurich. He completed his PhD at ETH Zurich in 2019, followed by postdoctoral training at the University of Zurich. His career has focused on developing several regenerative therapies following experimental stroke in mice. He now combines his experience in preclinical research with molecular and genetic tools to advance cell therapies for stroke toward the clinical stage. In his free time, Ruslan enjoys outdoor activities, travel, sports, and playing guitar.
